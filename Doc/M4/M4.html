Problem Statement

London’s youth transition challenge is urgent — and uneven

In the great work of GLA, London is a young city. The ambition is to make a difference in the lives of children and young people through services and projects tailored to their needs.

Challenge: more than one million young people across the UK are NEET, and London’s youth unemployment rate stands at 19.3% as of Q4 2024 — well above the national average and pre-pandemic levels.

The crisis falls disproportionately on groups facing systemic barriers, including young Black Londoners and disabled young people. National programmes are not always designed around London’s local barriers, borough variation, or community infrastructure.

Question: how might we tailor the Youth Offer for London to reach and support the greatest number of young people into sustained work or training, given that risk factors compound unevenly across different groups?

Say: “This is not just a youth unemployment problem. It is a targeting problem: where, who, and what support?”
Methodology

A practical pipeline from data to Youth Offer decisions

Filter London KS4 data→ Create transition risk proxy→ Compare boroughs→ Compare groups→ Explain risk factors→ Simulate interventions

What we deliberately did

  • Used borough-level evidence for place-based support.
  • Compared risk across disadvantaged, FSM, SEN and gender groups.
  • Kept the model explainable and policy-facing.
  • Used a simple what-if simulation to discuss interventions.

What we avoided

  • No individual young person risk scoring.
  • No claim that the proxy is confirmed NEET status.
  • No black-box “AI decides funding” framing.
  • No over-reliance on technical model diagnostics.
Say: “The point is not a flashy model. The point is a transparent way to target support.”

Why this proxy?

We used a cautious transition-risk proxy

Transition risk proxy: pupils not recorded as a sustained destination.

This is not the same as confirmed NEET status. It is an early warning signal that a young person is not clearly appearing in sustained education, employment or training after KS4.

QuestionAnswer
What does the classification prediction mean?It predicts whether a borough is above the London median for this transition-risk proxy.
What does it not mean?It does not predict that an individual young person will become NEET.
Why use it?It creates a clear triage layer for where the Youth Offer may need stronger support and follow-up.

Policy interpretation: this is a place-based prioritisation tool for GLA and borough teams, not an automated decision system.

Evidence

Disadvantage gaps highlight where universal averages miss need

Difference between disadvantaged pupils and pupils not known to be disadvantaged.

The “gap” is the difference in non-sustained destination rates between disadvantaged pupils and pupils not known to be disadvantaged.

Example: if disadvantaged pupils have a 9% non-sustained rate and other pupils have a 3% rate, the disadvantage gap is 6 percentage points.

This matters because a borough may look average overall, while disadvantaged pupils within that borough face much higher transition risk.

Evidence

Ethnicity patterns point to the need for trusted local partners

The ethnicity profile shows variation in non-sustained destination rates across broad groups.

This should not be used to stereotype individuals. It should guide where culturally relevant, trusted, community-based support may be needed.

Policy use: combine borough targeting with voluntary, community and grassroots organisations that already have trust with young people.

Interpretation

What the model adds: explanation, not automation

We used a simple scikit-learn model to ask which destination indicators best separate higher-risk from lower-risk borough profiles.

Permutation importance: sustained education is the strongest marker in the model.
SHAP: lower sustained education pushes boroughs towards the higher-risk class.

Plain-English finding: sustained education is the strongest marker of a lower-risk transition profile. Activity captured, employment and apprenticeships also matter.

Say carefully: “We are not saying education causes everything. We are saying sustained education is the strongest marker in this data.”

Proposed Solutions

A locally tailored, integrated Youth Offer pathway

Evidence from analysisRecommended Youth Offer response
Risk varies by borough.Place-based targeting: prioritise boroughs with higher transition risk.
Risk varies by group within boroughs.Group-sensitive support: tailor interventions for disadvantaged, FSM-eligible, SEN and EHC-plan pupils.
Sustained education is the strongest marker.Post-16 pathway support: strengthen school-to-college handovers, re-engagement and attendance follow-up.
Activity not captured matters.Tracking and outreach: improve data-sharing and use voluntary/community partners to find young people earlier.
Employment and apprenticeships matter.Employer brokerage: expand apprenticeship navigation, work experience and local employer links.

Recommendation: build on national employment policy by creating a locally tailored, integrated pathway designed around London’s specific barriers to youth employment.

Closing

From a general programme to targeted transition support

The Youth Offer should be targeted where transition risk is highest, but tailored according to which groups are driving that risk locally.

Place lens

Prioritise boroughs with higher transition risk.

Equity lens

Within those boroughs, focus on groups with the highest relative risk.
